# Domain-Agnostic Proof Verification via Structural Graph Learning on Synthetic Data

## Motivation

Existing verifiers like the pipeline in AdvancedMathBench rely on human expert annotations, which are domain-specific and expensive to acquire. This dependency prevents scaling to new domains such as geometry without costly reannotation. A verifier that can generalize across domains without fine-tuning would remove this bottleneck, but current methods fail because they learn domain-specific patterns rather than the underlying structural invariants of correct reasoning.

## Key Insight

The correctness of a proof is determined by the logical structure of its steps—such as valid substitution and transitivity—which are independent of the domain-specific symbols and can be learned from synthetic proofs generated from a formal theory.

## Method

### Domain-Agnostic Proof Verification via Structural Graph Learning on Synthetic Data

**What it is:** Structural Proof Verifier (SPV) – a Graph Neural Network (GNN) that encodes a proof as a directed acyclic graph (DAG) and predicts a binary correctness label. Input: a proof graph G=(V,E) where V are steps (each with a syntactic type from {axiom, deduction, conclusion}) and E are dependency edges (types: 'premise-of', 'derived-from'). Output: a scalar score s ∈ [0,1] denoting probability of correctness.

**How it works:**
```pseudocode
# Forward pass of SPV
Input: Proof graph G = (V,E), node features x_v (one-hot of dimension 3), edge features e_uv (one-hot of dimension 2)
Hyperparameters: hidden dim d=128, L=3, temperature τ=0.1
# 1. Node embedding:
for v in V:
    h_v^0 = W_emb * x_v   # W_emb ∈ R^{d×3}
# 2. Message passing (3 layers):
for l in 1..L:
    for v in V:
        m_v = sum_{u in N(v)} (W_msg * [h_u^{l-1}, e_uv])   # W_msg ∈ R^{d×(d+2)}
        h_v^l = ReLU(W_update * [h_v^{l-1}, m_v])           # W_update ∈ R^{d×(2d)}
# 3. Graph readout:
h_G = mean_{v in V} h_v^L
# 4. Classifier:
s = sigmoid(W_cls * h_G + b)   # W_cls ∈ R^{1×d}
return s
```
Training: Use InfoNCE contrastive loss with hard negative mining. For each correct proof in the batch (batch size 64), generate 15 incorrect variants by randomly replacing a step with a wrong type or swapping dependency edges. Additionally, generate synthetic proofs from a formal theory (group theory with 4 axioms and 3 inference rules: modus ponens, substitution, transitivity) where ground truth is known by construction. The synthetic data generator produces 10,000 correct proofs and 40,000 incorrect variants per domain. The model is trained for 100 epochs with Adam (lr=1e-4, weight decay=1e-5) on an A100 GPU (~2 hours per domain).

**Why this design:** We chose a GNN over a sequence model (e.g., Transformer) because proofs are inherently graph-structured; steps often refer to multiple prior steps, and a sequence cannot capture non-consecutive dependencies. The trade-off is higher computational cost and the need to define graph construction rules. We generate synthetic data from a formal theory rather than scraping human proofs because labels are noise-free and we can control domain-specific symbols (e.g., replace 'group operation' with generic 'op' to force structural learning). This comes at the cost of a potential domain gap: synthetic proofs may lack the variety of human-written geometry proofs. We use contrastive loss instead of binary cross-entropy because it encourages the model to learn discriminative features that separate correct from incorrect proofs by their structural differences, rather than by surface statistics. The downside is that contrastive loss requires careful selection of hard negatives to be effective.

**Why it measures what we claim:** The graph encoding (h_G) measures structural correctness because we assume that the logical validity of a proof is fully determined by the dependency relationships and the type-consistency of each step; this assumption fails when a proof is correct but uses an unconventional inference pattern not seen in training (e.g., a novel geometric construction), in which case the verifier may incorrectly classify it based on superficial similarity to training examples. The contrastive training with hard negatives measures the verifier's ability to distinguish structurally sound proofs from plausible but invalid ones; this works because hard negatives are generated by corrupting a correct proof's structure, forcing the model to learn the invariants of correct derivation. If the corruption method does not produce realistic errors (e.g., always introduces trivial mistakes), then the verifier may learn to detect only those artifacts rather than general structural validity. 
**Load-bearing assumption stated explicitly:** The logical validity of a proof is fully determined by the graph structure (node types and dependency edges), independent of domain-specific symbols. This assumption is verified empirically for the synthetic domains: we enumerate all syntactically correct DAGs of depth ≤5 in group theory and confirm that each corresponds to a valid proof. For deeper proofs, we rely on the generative process to guarantee that only structurally consistent DAGs are labeled correct. The gap between structural and semantic correctness (e.g., incorrect axiom instantiation) is not addressed by this method; the verifier focuses on structural invariants only, as motivated by the synthetic data generation which ensures that step content is consistent by construction.

## Contribution

(1) A graph-based proof verifier that trains on synthetic data from a formal domain and achieves zero-shot transfer to geometry and other domains without fine-tuning. (2) A synthetic data generation framework that produces labeled proof graphs with controlled difficulty and domain-agnostic symbol anonymization. (3) Empirical demonstration that structural proof features learned from algebra generalize to geometry, with analysis of the domain gap.

## Experiment

### Evaluation Setup

| Role | Choice | Rationale (≤12 words) |
|------|--------|----------------------|
| Dataset | Synthetic proof graphs from two formal theories: group theory (algebraic) and incidence geometry (geometric); each with 10,000 correct proofs and 40,000 incorrect variants | Tests generalization to unseen proof structures across domains |
| Primary metric | ROC-AUC | Threshold-agnostic ranking of correctness |
| Baseline 1 | Transformer (6 layers, hidden 256, Laplacian positional encoding) | Baseline ignoring graph dependencies, with comparable capacity |
| Baseline 2 | Rule-based verifier (modus ponens, substitution, transitivity via pattern matching) | Checks logical constraints, no learning |
| Baseline 3 | Graph feature MLP (global mean pool of node features + 2-layer MLP, hidden 256) | Tests value of message passing |
| Ablation-of-ours | Ours w/o message passing (same as Graph feature MLP) | Isolates effect of graph propagation |

### Why this setup validates the claim

The combination evaluates the claim that structural correctness can be learned from graph dependencies. Using synthetic proofs from two held-out domains ensures the method must generalize beyond training symbols, not just memorize. ROC-AUC directly measures ranking of correctness, capturing the verifier's confidence calibration. The Transformer baseline with positional encoding tests whether sequential context with positional information suffices; if ours outperforms, explicit graph structure is necessary. The rule-based verifier tests whether handcrafted rules match learned structural invariants; a gap shows learning captures patterns beyond explicit logic. The graph MLP (no message passing) tests the value of multi-hop propagation; outperformance confirms that aggregating distant nodes is key. The ablation isolates the contribution of message passing; if it degrades to the MLP level, propagation is essential. This design falsifies the claim if our method fails on structurally deep proofs or is no better than the MLP baseline.

### Expected outcome and causal chain

**vs. Transformer (seq)** — On a proof where two premises converge into a single conclusion (branching dependency), the Transformer linearizes the steps and loses this non-consecutive relation, misclassifying due to order ambiguity despite positional encoding. Our method encodes the graph directly, so it can aggregate information from both premises simultaneously via message passing. We expect a noticeable gap on proofs with high branching factor but parity on linear, chain-like proofs.

**vs. Rule-based verifier** — On a proof that uses a valid but unconventional inference pattern (e.g., a novel algebraic identity not in the rule set), the rule-based verifier rejects because its rules are incomplete. Our method has learned structural consistency from data and accepts if the graph dependencies are intact. We expect our method to have higher recall on rare inferences with correct structure, while the rule-based verifier has higher precision on common patterns. Overall ROC-AUC will be higher for ours.

**vs. Graph feature MLP** — On a deep proof requiring reasoning across 10+ hops (e.g., a long chain of deductions), the MLP aggregates all node features globally without considering order or pairwise dependencies, so it cannot distinguish a correct chain from a scrambled one. Our method's message passing propagates step-by-step along edges, preserving dependency order. We expect a large gap on proofs with sequential depth, with ours maintaining high accuracy while the MLP degrades to chance.

### What would falsify this idea

If our method performs no better than the graph MLP baseline on proofs with deep dependencies, or if it fails to distinguish structurally correct proofs from those with simple local corruptions (e.g., swapped step types) that the rule-based verifier catches, then the GNN is not learning structural validity as claimed.
