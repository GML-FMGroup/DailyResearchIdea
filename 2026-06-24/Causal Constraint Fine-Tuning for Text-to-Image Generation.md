# Causal Constraint Fine-Tuning for Text-to-Image Generation

## Motivation

The CF-World benchmark and CF-Eval reveal that state-of-the-art T2I models fail to generate images consistent with causal rules, particularly for implicit counterfactuals. However, existing approaches only evaluate causal reasoning post-hoc and do not incorporate causal constraints into the generation mechanism itself, leaving models free to produce causally inconsistent outputs. We address this by introducing a differentiable causal constraint loss that directly penalizes violations of causal rules during fine-tuning.

## Key Insight

By training a differentiable surrogate of a causal consistency evaluator, we can backpropagate through the diffusion process to minimize causal violations, effectively embedding causal rules into the model's weights.

## Method

**A) What it is:** Causal Constraint Fine-Tuning (CCFT) fine-tunes a pre-trained T2I diffusion model (e.g., Stable Diffusion) by adding a differentiable causal constraint loss that penalizes generated images that violate causal rules. Input: T2I model parameters θ, dataset D of factual prompt p and counterfactual prompt p' pairs (e.g., from CF-World). Output: Fine-tuned model parameters θ* with improved causal consistency.

**B) How it works:**
```python
# Pre-train surrogate evaluator
# Collect dataset: for each prompt, generate image latent z from diffusion model, compute CF-Eval score s (from VLM). Train MLP f_φ(z) -> s via MSE.
# MLP architecture: 2-layer MLP, hidden=256, GeLU activation. Train on 10,000 latent-score pairs, 500 epochs, lr=1e-3.

# Fine-tuning loop
for step in range(5000):
    for (p, p') in D:
        # Sample noise ε, generate latents z_p = denoise(ε, p; θ), z_p' = denoise(ε, p'; θ)
        # Compute surrogate scores
        s_p = f_φ(z_p), s_p' = f_φ(z_p')
        # Causal consistency loss: maximize s_p (factual should be high) and ensure s_p' < s_p by margin m=0.5
        L_causal = -s_p + max(0, s_p' - s_p + 0.5)
        # Also standard image loss (e.g., L_simple from diffusion)
        L_total = L_simple + 0.1 * L_causal
        Update θ via gradient descent (lr=1e-5)
    # Every 1000 steps, retrain f_φ on fresh latent-score pairs from current model
    if step % 1000 == 0 and step > 0:
        Re-collect 10,000 latent-score pairs from current θ; re-train f_φ for 100 epochs.
```

**C) Why this design:** We chose a surrogate evaluator (MLP on latents) over directly using the VLM (e.g., MiniGPT-4) because the VLM is not differentiable, forcing us to trade off the evaluator's accuracy for differentiability; the surrogate approximates the VLM score but introduces approximation error. We fine-tune all diffusion parameters (rather than only a text encoder or adapter) because causal reasoning may require modifying the entire generation pipeline, accepting the computational cost of full fine-tuning. We use paired factual-counterfactual prompts with a contrastive margin loss (rather than a single-prompt target score) because causal consistency requires that an intervention on the prompt produces a commensurate change in the image; this margin formulation enforces that the factual image scores higher than the counterfactual under the evaluator, directly operationalizing the notion that a causal rule violation lowers the score. The hyperparameters m=0.5 and λ=0.1 were chosen to balance penalty strength with training stability; a larger m might over-penalize but a smaller m may be insufficient. Periodic surrogate retraining mitigates distribution shift but incurs extra compute (each retraining: ~1 hour on 1 GPU).

**D) Why it measures what we claim:** The surrogate score f_φ(z) measures causal consistency because it is trained to predict CF-Eval's score, which is designed by the authors of the CF-World benchmark to quantify whether a generated image respects the causal rule implied by the prompt; the assumption is that CF-Eval's VLM-based judgments are a valid proxy for causal rule adherence. **Load-bearing assumption:** The surrogate evaluator f_φ(z) remains accurate for evaluating causal consistency of images generated by the fine-tuned model despite distribution shift. This assumption fails when the VLM misinterprets the prompt (e.g., ambiguous spatial relations), in which case the surrogate reflects the VLM's bias rather than true causal consistency, or when the fine-tuned model produces latents that the surrogate incorrectly scores high (reward hacking). Periodic retraining of the surrogate on fresh model outputs directly addresses the second failure mode. The contrastive loss term max(0, s_p' - s_p + m) measures causal necessity: it requires that the counterfactual image (p') has a lower causal consistency score than the factual image (p) by at least m. This operationalizes the idea that changing a causal factor should reduce the image's consistency with the original causal rule. The underlying assumption is that p and p' differ only in the critical causal variable (e.g., with/without electricity) and that CF-Eval correctly penalizes violations of that variable. If the prompt pair inadvertently changes multiple attributes, the loss may penalize irrelevant differences, causing the model to overfit to spurious correlations.

## Contribution

(1) A differentiable causal constraint loss for fine-tuning T2I models that directly penalizes violations of causal rules during generation, using a surrogate trained to approximate a VLM-based causal consistency evaluator. (2) A framework for training a surrogate evaluator that maps diffusion latents to causal consistency scores, enabling end-to-end backpropagation through the generation process. (3) Empirical demonstration that CCFT improves causal reasoning performance on the CF-World benchmark over baseline T2I models, closing the gap between evaluation and generation.

## Experiment

### Evaluation Setup

| Role | Choice | Rationale (≤12 words) |
|------|--------|-----------------------|
| Dataset | CF-World | Designed for causal reasoning in T2I. |
| Primary metric | CF-Eval score | Directly measures causal consistency. |
| Baseline 1 | Base Stable Diffusion | Untuned model; tests improvement from fine-tuning. |
| Baseline 2 | Standard fine-tuning | Fine-tune with only L_simple; tests causal loss effect. |
| Baseline 3 | DALL-E 3 | Strong closed-source model; tests against SOTA. |
| Ablation-of-ours | CCFT w/o margin loss | Tests necessity of contrastive formulation. |
| Calibration set | CF-World validation split (200 prompts) | Periodically retrain surrogate to avoid reward hacking. |

### Why this setup validates the claim

The setup directly tests the central claim that CCFT improves causal reasoning in T2I generation. Using CF-World, which provides paired factual-counterfactual prompts, the CF-Eval score serves as a direct measure of causal consistency. Baseline 1 (Base SD) establishes the initial gap. Baseline 2 (Standard FT) isolates whether simple fine-tuning without causal constraint suffices—if causal rules are already learned, standard FT would also improve, but we predict it will not because it lacks causal constraint. Baseline 3 (DALL-E 3) sets an upper bound from a highly capable closed-source model. The ablation (no margin) tests whether the contrastive formulation is essential. The calibration set is used to periodically retrain the surrogate evaluator on fresh model outputs, ensuring that the surrogate remains accurate despite distribution shift and preventing reward hacking. If CCFT outperforms all baselines and ablation, the claim is supported. Conversely, if the ablation matches CCFT, then the contrastive margin is redundant; if CCFT fails to beat standard FT, then the causal constraint is ineffective.

### Expected outcome and causal chain

**vs. Base Stable Diffusion** — On a case where the prompt describes a causal rule (e.g., "a light bulb that is on" vs "a light bulb that is off" with same background), the base model generates images that differ only in the bulb brightness but may not reflect the causal relation (e.g., still shows a lit room). Because it lacks any causal consistency objective, it treats both prompts similarly. Our method fine-tunes with a contrastive loss that forces the counterfactual image to score lower on CF-Eval, so we expect a large improvement in CF-Eval score on counterfactual prompts (e.g., >20% relative), with a noticeable gap on subsets requiring causal reasoning.

**vs. Standard fine-tuning** — On a case where fine-tuning only on L_simple may produce high factual scores but similar counterfactual scores (e.g., both images are plausible but the counterfactual doesn't violate the causal rule). Standard FT lacks the constraint to reduce counterfactual consistency. Our method explicitly penalizes high counterfactual scores via the margin loss, so we expect our model to have a larger margin between factual and counterfactual scores, leading to higher CF-Eval overall, especially on counterfactual prompts (e.g., 10-15% better than standard FT on counterfactuals).

**vs. DALL-E 3** — On a case involving implicit counterfactuals (e.g., "a vase on a table" vs "the vase fell off the table"), DALL-E 3 may generate plausible images but often fails to enforce the causal consequence (e.g., the vase might still be intact). This is because it relies on broad training but lacks targeted causal fine-tuning. Our method directly addresses such failures by training on paired counterfactuals, so we expect our model to surpass DALL-E 3 on hard counterfactual subsets (e.g., +5-10% on implicit counterfactuals), while DALL-E 3 may still excel on easy factual prompts.

**vs. Calibration (no retraining)** — Without periodic retraining, the surrogate may be exploited by the fine-tuned model, leading to high CF-Eval scores that do not reflect true causal consistency. We expect that the periodic retraining variant will maintain a stable correlation between surrogate and true CF-Eval scores, while the variant without retraining will show a growing divergence over training steps, eventually causing overestimation of causal ability.

### What would falsify this idea

If CCFT does not outperform standard fine-tuning on counterfactual CF-Eval scores, or if the gains are uniform across all subsets (rather than concentrated on counterfactual prompts where causal reasoning is needed), then the central claim that causal constraint fine-tuning improves causal reasoning is falsified. Additionally, if the periodic retraining does not prevent reward hacking (i.e., CF-Eval scores rise but human rating of causal consistency does not), then the surrogate-based approach is fundamentally flawed.
