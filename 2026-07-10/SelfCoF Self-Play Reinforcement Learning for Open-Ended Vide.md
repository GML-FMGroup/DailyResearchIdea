# SelfCoF: Self-Play Reinforcement Learning for Open-Ended Video Reasoning Generation

## Motivation

OpenCoF (the specific prior work) fine-tunes a video generation model on a static dataset of 17K reasoning tasks, which inherently limits coverage to 11 task families. This supervised paradigm cannot generate or learn from reasoning challenges outside the curated set, bottlenecking generalization to novel visual-temporal reasoning problems. We hypothesize that the model itself can bootstrap its own training data by generating open-ended reasoning challenges and learning from outcome rewards.

## Key Insight

Self-play enables the model to explore the space of visual reasoning problems autonomously, where successful reasoning chains are self-rewarding, eliminating the coverage bottleneck of static datasets.

## Method

We propose SelfCoF, a self-play reinforcement learning framework for video reasoning generation. **What it is:** SelfCoF trains a video generation model (e.g., Wan2.2) to propose open-ended reasoning challenges and solve them, learning from a reward model that verifies solution correctness. **How it works:**

```python
# SelfCoF Training Loop
Initialize video generation model M (with reasoning tokens)  # e.g., Wan2.2-I2V-A14B (full-scale) or Wan2.2-I2V-3B (initial validation; training: ~200 GPU hours on 8 A100s)
Initialize reward model R = 3-layer Transformer, 8 attention heads, hidden=512, trained via cross-entropy on seed dataset of 1K chains (500 human-verified, 500 rule-based templates) with Adam (lr=1e-4, 50 epochs)
Initialize replay buffer B (capacity N=10000)
For iteration = 1 to K:
    # Problem generation: sample diverse initial frame
    I = sample from a pool of random still images (e.g., from DALL-E or random videos)  # diversity
    Q = generate_query(M, I)  # text description of reasoning goal; generated by M conditioned on I
    # Solution generation
    F, T = generate_chain(M, I, Q)  # F: sequence of frames, T: reasoning tokens
    # Reward assignment
    r = R(F, Q)  # scalar outcome reward (binary correctness logit)
    Store (I, Q, F, T, r) in B
    # Policy update (PPO)
    Sample batch of size 64 from B
    Update M with PPO to maximize expected reward, with KL penalty λ=0.01 to prevent policy collapse
    # Periodic reward model calibration (every 10000 iterations)
    Evaluate R on held-out set of 500 human-verified chains; if accuracy < 85%, fine-tune R on held-out set for 10 epochs (lr=1e-5)
```

**Why this design:** 
Three key design decisions: (1) We use a **learned reward model** instead of human feedback to enable scalable, automatic reward assignment during self-play, at the cost of potential reward model inaccuracies that may misguide learning; to mitigate this, we periodically calibrate R against human-verified chains. (2) We choose **PPO** over simpler REINFORCE because PPO's clipped surrogate objective stabilizes training by limiting policy updates, despite requiring two critic networks and additional hyperparameters. (3) We **sample diverse initial frames** from a large pool rather than using a fixed set—this promotes coverage of diverse visual contexts but risks generating problems that are too easy or too hard; we mitigate this by early stopping generation if the reward variance is low. In contrast to prior self-play methods like ReAct (which operate in text environments), SelfCoF is the first to apply self-play to video generation reasoning, where the visual-temporal chain requires both generative and reasoning capabilities.

**Why it measures what we claim:**
The reward r from R measures **reasoning correctness** under assumption A: R's decision boundary aligns with human judgment of reasoning validity across diverse visual scenarios. R is trained on 500 human-verified and 500 synthesized chains, but failure mode F occurs when adversarial or out-of-distribution reasoning chains exploit spurious correlations, causing r to reflect R's biases rather than true correctness. The KL penalty λ=0.01 in PPO mitigates F by preventing M from deviating far from its initial distribution, reducing exploitation of R's imperfections. Additionally, periodic calibration of R on human-verified chains (every 10000 iterations) attempts to maintain alignment. The **PPO clipping mechanism** measures **policy improvement stability** by limiting per-step update size; this assumes that small updates preserve generative diversity, and fails when updates are too conservative, stalling learning.

## Contribution

(1) A self-play reinforcement learning framework for video reasoning generation that eliminates the need for curated supervised datasets by having the model propose its own open-ended challenges. (2) A demonstration that self-generated problems lead to improved generalization on unseen reasoning tasks compared to supervised fine-tuning on a closed dataset. (3) A 1K seed dataset of correct/incorrect video reasoning chains for training the initial reward model.

## Experiment

### Evaluation Setup

| Role | Choice | Rationale (≤12 words) |
|------|--------|-----------------------|
| Dataset | CLEVRER | Standard video reasoning benchmark |
| Primary metric | Reasoning Accuracy | Measures correctness of reasoning chain |
| Baseline 1 | Wan2.2-I2V-A14B | No reasoning tokens or self-play |
| Baseline 2 | Supervised CoF | Same architecture but supervised on seed data |
| Ablation | SelfCoF (fixed frames) | Removes initial frame diversity |

### Why this setup validates the claim
CLEVRER requires multi-step temporal and causal reasoning, directly testing the quality of generated reasoning chains. Wan2.2 isolates the effect of reasoning tokens and self-play; Supervised CoF isolates the benefit of self-play over static supervision; the ablation tests the importance of diverse problem generation. The primary metric, reasoning accuracy (proportion of correct answers based on ground-truth annotations), directly measures whether the generated video correctly embodies the reasoning process. This combination forms a falsifiable test because if self-play and diversity are crucial, we expect clear performance gaps on complex or novel scenarios.

### Expected outcome and causal chain

**vs. Wan2.2-I2V-A14B** — On a case where a causal chain requires three steps (e.g., object A pushes B, B hits C, C falls), the baseline, lacking reasoning tokens, generates a video where the causal sequence is incoherent (e.g., B moves before A pushes). Our method, with reasoning tokens and self-play, learns to plan the sequence step-by-step, generating a temporally correct video. Thus, we expect a large accuracy gap on multi-step questions and parity on single-step ones.

**vs. Supervised CoF** — On a case with a novel reasoning pattern not in the seed dataset, the supervised model overfits and produces an incorrect chain (e.g., fails to reason about counterfactuals). Our self-play method, by generating diverse problems and learning from its own solutions, generalizes to such novel patterns. We expect a noticeable gap on out-of-distribution examples but similar performance on in-distribution ones.

**vs. SelfCoF (fixed frames)** — On a case where the fixed initial frame is too simple (e.g., single object), the ablated model cannot generate challenging problems, leading to poor reasoning on complex queries. Our full method, with diverse frames, automatically increases difficulty, producing a curriculum. Hence, we expect the full model to achieve higher overall accuracy, especially on complex reasoning tasks.

### What would falsify this idea
If the full SelfCoF does not significantly outperform the supervised baseline on out-of-distribution CLEVRER questions, or if the ablation with fixed frames achieves comparable accuracy, then the central claim that self-play with diverse problem generation is essential for reasoning generalization would be falsified.
